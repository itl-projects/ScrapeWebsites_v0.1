This determines how effectively the vehicle can be braked on wet roads. Class A is the best and Class F is the worst. Classes D and G are not used. Tires with good wet grip have shorter braking distance and more stable driving characteristics. The difference in braking distance is four car lengths. For example, the difference in braking distance up to just over 18 meters - or four car lengths - between the tire of Class A and the tire of Class F. The difference in braking distance between tires that are in the classes one after another is a car length (3-6 meters). »Best and worst. Class A has more than 18 meters shorter braking distance than Class G. » Classes in succession, for example B-C, have a difference in the braking distance of one car length. Wet grip classification gives incorrect signalsThe wet grip classification does not show how good a winter tire is under Nordic conditions. Nordic stud-free winter tires are designed for Nordic winter conditions with a lot of smooth ice and snow. In stud-free winter tires for central European conditions, high-speed driving characteristics on dry roads are prioritized at the expense of the ice grip.
